Biomolecular solvation

Although the properties of bulk water are well characterized, our understanding of the structure and properties of biomolecular solvation remains lacking. A better description of water structure within protein crystals would allow us to improve the refinement of protein crystal structures. In this project, we generate the water structure within a protein unit cell and compare it with the experimentally-derived water density. We illustrate that a better description of water in the unit cell would improve the protein structures we obtain from diffraction data, and we benchmark water models commonly used for biomolecular simulations.

  • Altan, I., Fusco, D., Afonine, P.V. & Charbonneau, P. (2018). Learning about Biomolecular Solvation from Water in Protein Crystals. The Journal of Physical Chemistry B, 122(9), 2475-2486.

Computational protein crystallization

We aim to understand the physicochemical processes that govern protein crystals by using soft matter simulation and theory. Modeling globular proteins as hard spheres with attractive patches allows us to recapitulate the protein phase diagram. Constructing these patchy models starting from the microscopic details of protein-protein interactions allows us to determine the extent to which such microscopic details affect the physics of self-assembly. Using this approach, we attempt to determine the microscopic origin of inverted solubility (i.e. melting when the system is cooled down) in a human eye lens protein, the mutation of which can cause cataracts.

  • Altan, I., Khan, A., James, S., Quinn, M.K., McManus, J.J., & Charbonneau, P. (2019) Using schematic models to understand the microscopic basis for inverted solubility in γD-crystallin. The Journal of Physical Chemistry B, 123(47), 10061-10072.
  • Khan, A. R., James, S., Quinn, M. K., Altan, I., Charbonneau, P., & McManus, J. J. (2019). Temperature-dependent interactions explain normal and inverted solubility in a γD-crystallin mutant. Biophysical Journal.
  • Altan, I. & Charbonneau, P. (2019) Obtaining soft matter models of proteins and their phase behavior. Protein self-assembly: methods and protocols. pp. 209-227. Humana, New York, NY.
  • Altan, I., Charbonneau, P., & Snell, E. H. (2016). Computational crystallization. Archives of biochemistry and biophysics, 602, 12-20.

Protein self-assembly in squids

For my postdoc work, I am utilizing patchy particle simulations and theory to understand how S-crystallins, squid eye lens proteins, self-assemble to form a robustly transparent, spherically graded eye lens. I also study reflectin proteins, which are responsible for many optical effects in cephalopods, such as reflectivity and iridescence, to elucidate how these optical devices self-assemble.

Teaching science through cooking

During my Ph.D., I taught a "Chemistry and Physics of Cooking" class with my Ph.D. advisor, aimed at introducing soft matter physics to first year students through cooking. I have also been part of outreach activities for elementry school students, demonstrating chemistry and physics concepts through cooking.
